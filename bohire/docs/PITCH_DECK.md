## Slide 2 — The Problem

**Visual:** Split screen or timeline. Left: a frustrated job seeker hitting "Apply" 1,000 times. Right: a recruiter drowning in identical PDF resumes, inbox flooded. Center stat in bold: "1,000 AI-generated applications in 6 hours."

### Copy

**The resume is dead. Nobody told recruiting.**

- AI tools generate polished resumes in 30 seconds
- A single job post now receives 500–2,000 applications in 48 hours
- Recruiters can't tell a real candidate from an AI-generated one
- 72% of resumes never reach a human — filtered by ATS trained on keywords, not capability
- Candidates: burned out from spray-and-pray
- Recruiters: overwhelmed, missing great candidates in the noise

**The signal is gone. The process is broken for everyone.**

**Speaker Notes:**
> "This isn't a small frustration. Since GPT-3 hit consumer apps, resume volume has gone up 3-5x at most companies. But the quality signal has collapsed. Recruiters are spending more time reading, making worse decisions. Candidates are sending more applications, getting fewer responses. The whole system is misaligned. And the core tool — the resume — was never designed for this world."

## Slide 3 — The Old Way

**Visual:** A painful flowchart. Resume → ATS (labeled "Black Hole 🕳️") → Phone screen (maybe) → 4–5 interviews → Reference checks → Offer → Wrong hire. Arrows looping back with annotations like "Ghosted," "No feedback," "6 weeks wasted."

### Copy

**The standard hiring process, 2024:**

1. Candidate submits resume (usually via 45-min application form)
2. ATS discards 70% based on keyword match
3. Recruiter screens remaining 30% — often in <10 seconds per resume
4. 3–5 interview rounds over 4–6 weeks
5. Offer extended to someone who looked good on paper
6. 30–50% of technical hires fail to meet expectations within 90 days

**Average time-to-fill: 44 days. Average cost-per-hire: $4,700.**
**And it still doesn't tell you if they can actually do the job.**

**Speaker Notes:**
> "The resume process was designed in an era of physical mail and typed CVs. Every layer — ATS, keyword filtering, phone screens — is a patch on a fundamentally broken system. And the output is bad. Companies still make the wrong hire all the time. They just spent 6 weeks and $5K finding out."

---

## Slide 4 — The New Way

**Visual:** Clean four-step flow, horizontal. Each step has a small icon:
1. 🔗 Connect GitHub (or Figma, Notion, Salesforce)
2. 🤖 AI analyzes actual work
3. ✅ Verified profile generated (+ World ID badge)
4. 🔔 Recruiter requests a "bolo" → Candidate approves/denies

Arrows are clean, modern. The vibe is: *this is obviously the future.*

### Copy

**BoHire replaces the resume with verified proof of work.**

1. **Connect:** Candidate links GitHub (or Figma, Notion, etc.) — no resume required
2. **Analyze:** AI reviews actual code, projects, contribution patterns
3. **Verify:** World ID proves they're a real human. Profile is locked.
4. **Bolo:** Recruiter requests permission to view. Candidate approves or denies. Instant revocation.

**Candidates own their data. Recruiters get signal, not noise.**
**No more black holes. No more spam. Both sides win.**

**Speaker Notes:**
> "We call the permission request a 'bolo' — it's built on the Bolospot protocol, which handles consent and access control at the data layer. The key insight is that the candidate is in control. A recruiter can't just pull your profile. They have to ask. And you can revoke access at any time. That changes the relationship entirely — from 'you're a commodity in someone's ATS' to 'your profile is your asset.'"

## Slide 5 — Product

**Visual:** Three-panel product screenshot or mockup:
- Panel 1: Candidate's BoHire profile — photo, name, verified badge (World ID checkmark), top languages (Python: Expert, TypeScript: Advanced), project highlights, AI-generated assessment ("Strong systems thinker, consistent contributor to open source, writes readable code under deadline")
- Panel 2: AI assessment view — scored dimensions: Code Quality, Collaboration, Depth, Growth Trajectory
- Panel 3: Bolo request flow — recruiter sends request with note → candidate sees it → one tap approve/deny → approved view unlocks

### Copy

**What a recruiter sees:**

- Verified identity (World ID — they're a real person)
- Verified work (GitHub-analyzed, not self-reported)
- AI assessment (signal, not score — we tell you *how* they think)
- Permission trail (who saw it, when, what they can access)

**What a candidate controls:**
- Who can see their profile
- What level of detail is shared
- Instant revocation with one tap

**Speaker Notes:**
> "The AI assessment is not a score out of 10. We spent a lot of time on this. Scores are gameable, and they're reductive. Instead, we surface qualitative signals — is this person a consistent contributor or a weekend sprint coder? Do they write tests? Do their PRs get merged or revised? Do they build in public or work in private repos? These things matter and they're observable. We surface them in plain language that a recruiter can actually use."

**Visual:** Three icons in a row:
- 🤖 AI made resumes worthless (text over collage of AI-generated resume templates)
- 🌍 World ID makes global verification possible (World ID orb image)
- 🔑 Agents need permission layers (key + lock icon, subtle reference to agentic future)

### Copy

**Three things converged:**

**1. AI destroyed the resume as a trust signal**
> GPT-4 writes better resumes than most candidates can. The signal is gone.

**2. Real-world identity verification is now consumer-grade**
> World ID from Tools for Humanity verifies you're a real human, globally, privately. This didn't exist 3 years ago.

**3. The agentic hiring future requires permission infrastructure**
> Agents will soon apply for jobs. Agents will soon screen candidates. The hiring process needs a permission layer. BoHire is that layer.

**The window is open. It won't stay open long.**

**Speaker Notes:**
> "The timing isn't accidental. The resume worked because it was hard to fake. Then AI made it easy. World ID gives us the verification backbone that didn't exist before. And the agentic wave — autonomous agents applying for jobs, screening candidates — is coming faster than most people expect. When that happens, you need a trust layer underneath. Bolospot is that protocol. BoHire is the first consumer product built on it."

## Slide 8 — Business Model

**Visual:** Three pricing tiers shown as clean cards with a clear arrow pointing right toward Enterprise. Emphasis on "Candidates Always Free" as a separate callout above.

### Copy

**Candidates: always free. Recruiters: pay for access.**

| Tier           | Price         | For                                   |
|---------------|--------------|----------------------------------------|
| **Starter**   | $299/mo       | Small teams, 50 bolo requests/mo       |
| **Growth**    | $799/mo       | Growing companies, 5 seats, 200/mo     |
| **Enterprise**| $3K–$15K/mo   | Agencies, large companies, API access  |

**Why this model works:**
- Candidate supply (free) → recruiter demand → pays for itself
- $299/mo is one bad hire's cost in 2 hours of wasted interviews
- Net revenue retention >110% as teams expand seats
- Gross margin: ~78% at scale

**Long-term:** Enterprise API licensing to ATS platforms (Greenhouse, Lever, Ashby)

**Speaker Notes:**
> "The free candidate side is non-negotiable. It's not a freemium trick — it's a philosophical commitment. Candidates have been exploited by hiring platforms for decades. We're on their side. That said, the recruiter side has strong willingness to pay. LinkedIn Recruiter is $10,000+/seat/year and recruiters pay it while complaining the whole time. We're $299/mo and we give them verified humans. The value equation is obvious."

And the permission layer — the bolo — isn't just a feature. It's a philosophical shift in who owns your data. That's not a feature LinkedIn can ship in a quarter."
